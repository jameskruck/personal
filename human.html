Introduction

Understanding human behaviour is essential for leaders, educators, and anyone who creates for or works with people. This collection of resources explores the complexities of human interaction, decision-making, and communication, offering valuable insights to inspire thoughtful leadership and effective collaboration.

From Brené Brown's work on vulnerability and empathy in leadership to Daniel Kahneman's exploration of how we think, these books, podcasts, and ideas challenge us to better understand ourselves and others. Whether you're looking to improve team dynamics, connect meaningfully with others, or critically evaluate popular ideas, these sources provide a rich foundation for reflection and growth.

Dare to Lead

Book

Brené Brown

Why you might like it

Brené Brown has written several books that are valuable for leaders, but this one stood out with key takeaways that really resonated with me. I particularly appreciated her concept of "rumbling with vulnerability"—the practice of addressing unspoken emotions within a team and creating a safe space for open dialogue. Her definition of empathy also struck a chord: it's about connecting with the emotions that drive an experience, not just the experience itself. And finally, the project management idea of "Painting Done": asking, "What will this look like to you when I've delivered exactly what you're asking for?"

Supercommunicators

Book

Charles Duhigg

Why you might like it

In Supercommunicators, author Charles Duhigg explores why some people excel at talking to anyone. He offers practical insights, like embracing the fact that we are complex individuals with many overlapping identities (father, lover of Cicero, weekend cyclist) and how these identities create bridges to connect with others. He also discusses the idea of recognizing the type of conversation you're in: Are you talking to someone who wants to be helped, hugged, or heard?

If you want a primer on the book, Duhigg appeared on an episode of You Are Not So Smart.

Nexus: A Brief History of Information Networks from the Stone Age to AI

Book

Yuval Noah Harari

Why you might like it

I'm still early in my reading of this book, but so far I've been really intrigued by some of the ideas. A key thesis in this book is that information has not been used throughout human history to identify the truth. That is, the author identifies a naive view of information, one that supposes that the more information that a person has, the closer to the truth they will be.

Thinking, Fast and Slow

Book

Daniel Kahneman

Why you might like it

Despite falling to the Replication Crisis that swept Psychology in the 2010s, I thought this book raised a lot of interesting possibilities. Kahneman's overall thesis is that the human mind has two systems: system 1 reacts, system 2 tries to reason through a situation.

As an aside, I found Kahneman's presentation of System 1 very similar to the Stoic theory of action, which supposes that humans always react to their stimuli with no decision making involved at all (that is to say, the decision is made as soon as the stimuli is assessed).

If Books Could Kill

Podcast

Michael Hobbes and Peter Shamshiri

Why you might like it

Sometimes I read a book that really resonates with me - until I read the counterargument. If Books Could Kill is hosted by Michael Hobbes and Peter Shamshiri. Each episode, the hosts critique the "airport bestsellers that captured our hearts and ruined our minds."
