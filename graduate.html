Introduction

Graduate education plays a critical role in shaping future scholars, educators, and professionals, but it also faces significant challenges and opportunities for improvement. This collection brings together resources that explore various aspects of graduate school, from critiques of its current structure to strategies for enhancing Teaching Assistant (TA) development.

Whether you're interested in rethinking doctoral education, designing effective TA training programs, or exploring the unique challenges of online and blended learning for TAs, these resources offer research-based insights and practical recommendations. They provide a foundation for creating more supportive, inclusive, and impactful graduate education experiences.

Working Theories for Teaching Assistant Development

Book

Greta Gorsuch, Editor

Why you might like it

Gorsuch's edited volume provides both theoretical frameworks and practical research findings that can inform the strategic planning and implementation of graduate TA programs, particularly in the areas of TA development, international TA development, and faculty development.

The book goes beyond good practices and delves into the research underpinning effective TA development. Importantly, the book:

  • Provides a framework for understanding how TA's learn.
  • Offers insights into effective TA professional development and supervision.
  • Provides practical examples of instructional resources and activities.

Working Theories for Teaching Assistant Development equips readers with the knowledge, understanding, and tools to create and implement effective, research-informed TA development initiatives.

What Contributes to Teaching Assistant Development: Differential Responses to Key Design Features

Research Article

P.L. Hardré and A.O. Burris

Why you might like it

This article explores how TAs perceive the design and content of professional development programs. The researchers investigated the perceptions of 210 TAs who participated in a two-day, institutionally mandated program called the All-Campus Teaching Assistant Orientation and Training (ATA) program at a research university.

The study focused on the following research questions:

  • What are TAs' perceptions of, and responses to, various components of a two-day professional development event?
  • How do TAs perceive that global design elements of a training event contribute to their learning and development?
  • What percentage of TAs chose to invest in an online pre-event introduction to the professional development content, and how does that participation link to their perceptions of the on-site activities?
  • What percentage of TAs plan to continue investing in learning to teach?
  • What do TAs perceive as more important skills and strategies among diverse offerings, and how do their perceptions reflect their needs and interests?

This source also provides several practical recommendations for designing TA professional development programs, including:

  • Involving TAs in the design and planning of training programs.
  • Emphasising practical strategies that TAs can use in their teaching.
  • Providing TAs with opportunities to practice their teaching skills and receive feedback.
  • Creating a supportive environment for TA development that values teaching and encourages collaboration.

Teaching Assistant Development and Contributions in Online, MOOC and Blended Synchronous Settings: An Integrative Review

Research Article

M.L. Wadams and K. Schick-Makaroff

Why you might like it

This article provides practical guidance for instructors, TAs, and administrators involved in planning and delivering online, blended synchronous learning, and MOOCs.

This article is particularly useful for TA training because it offers:

  • A taxonomy of TA roles and responsibilities across these three modalities presented as pragmatic checklists for instructors and TAs.
  • Strategies to prepare TAs to teach across the three modalities, presented as pragmatic checklists for instructors and administrators.

The authors argue that "on-the-job" TA training is not sufficient and that more resources should be provided to prepare TAs for their diverse roles and responsibilities. They suggest that professional development programs should be created to help TAs:

  • Learn and receive training about online teaching, which may also change their potentially negative perceptions of remote teaching and learning.
  • Develop the skills needed to manage the cognitive overload and communication interruptions associated with blended synchronous learning.
